# Accountability Classification Knowledge Base
# For Non-Converted Demos — Sales / Product / Consumer

## Purpose
Every demo that results in "Not Converted" must be classified by accountability. This classification drives team learning, tutor improvement, and sales coaching. It is one of the most consequential outputs this agent produces. Be precise. Cite evidence. Do not guess.

---

## SALES ACCOUNTABILITY
The conversion failed primarily because of how the sales process was managed, not because of the demo quality or the parent's circumstances.

### Indicators
- Decision maker (usually the parent) was not present during the demo
- Intent was not checked before giving a free demo (paid demo should have been offered)
- Commitment was not secured immediately after the demo
- Follow-up was delayed or insufficient
- Closing was done at the wrong time (too early or too long after demo)
- Payment was not qualified before demo (parent had budget issues that should have been discovered earlier)
- Rapport was not built before or during the closing attempt
- Student's readiness to commit was misjudged
- Agent gave too much time/flexibility without securing commitment

### Real Examples from Data
- "Did not include the decision maker — gave permission to book 4 hours without asking for commitment" → SALES
- "Should have been a paid demo to test intent — allowed time to think which caused ghosting" → SALES
- "Closing was done after taking feedback, shouldnt have prolonged it" → SALES
- "Did not correctly identify the decision maker, pushed on ticket size" → SALES
- "Weak closing, misunderstood their meaning — no commitment taken, kid ghosted" → SALES

---

## PRODUCT ACCOUNTABILITY
The conversion failed primarily because the wrong teacher was allocated, or the demo was not properly curated for the student's actual needs.

### Indicators
- Teacher accent/communication style was a mismatch for the student's stated preferences
- Wrong curriculum board or level — teacher used Cambridge materials for Edexcel student
- Teacher did not follow demo instructions/learning objectives provided
- Resource allocation was poor — better teacher available who would have matched better
- Demo was not curated to the student's specific pain points (teacher went generic)
- Teacher had documented issues (pace, engagement) that were known but ignored in allocation
- Teacher gave a lectured demo when student required interactive session

### Real Examples from Data
- "Incorrect resource allocation — Absence of fluency/ability to communicate an idea in English — Mismatch between kid's academic standing and teaching methodology" → PRODUCT
- "Wrong resource allocation — Nageena's English demos have accent/pronunciation issues" → PRODUCT
- "Resource allocation issue — needed someone like mahnoor/meher accent" → PRODUCT
- "Tutor was specifically instructed about demo LO, but did not follow through" → PRODUCT
- "Slow paced demo which got very boring for the student — incorrect resource allocation" → PRODUCT
- "Tutor had camera off — this impacts demos" → PRODUCT (partially)

---

## CONSUMER ACCOUNTABILITY
The conversion failed because of the parent/student's own circumstances, preferences, or decisions — not because of sales or product failures.

### Indicators
- Parent explicitly chose face-to-face tutoring after a good demo
- Parent chose a cheaper option that Tuitional cannot match at comparable quality
- Parent is price-sensitive beyond Tuitional's minimum viable rate
- Student is genuinely not ready or is self-directed
- External circumstances (travel, exams postponed, budget changed)
- Parent is testing multiple providers and chose another after fair comparison
- Student ghosted without any indication of issue (no sales or product red flags)

### Real Examples from Data
- "Demo was good, they chose face-to-face classes" → CONSUMER
- "They had a better fit tutor elsewhere, not that ours wasn't good" → CONSUMER
- "Student is travelling, will start after mock exams" → CONSUMER
- "Parent decided to teach the kid himself" → CONSUMER (but note: also has sales element — agent pushed on ticket size)
- "Student decided to take group sessions with friends instead" → CONSUMER

---

## MIXED ACCOUNTABILITY
Two categories are both genuinely responsible. Use this when evidence supports both clearly.

### When to Use Mixed
- Sales agent did not qualify intent AND teacher had a known pace issue (SALES + PRODUCT)
- Teacher was a reasonable match but sales agent closed too early AND parent had budget concerns not discovered (SALES + CONSUMER)

### Format for Mixed
State both categories and the evidence for each:
"MIXED: Sales (agent did not identify decision maker) + Product (tutor used wrong curriculum board despite being briefed)"

---

## Confidence Levels
- **High**: Clear evidence from sales comments directly supports the classification
- **Medium**: Evidence is present but could be interpreted differently
- **Low**: Evidence is insufficient; classification is a best estimate — flag for Dawood review

---

## Classification Traps to Avoid
1. **Do not classify as Consumer when a known product issue exists** — if the parent said "accent was a problem" and the teacher had documented accent issues, that is Product, not Consumer.
2. **Do not classify as Product when the agent simply gave a bad demo** — a single bad demo without resource/allocation errors is Sales (should have been a paid demo) or potentially Consumer.
3. **Do not classify as Sales when the parent was genuinely price-limited** — if no amount of good follow-up would have closed this lead, it is Consumer.
4. **Always cite specific text from sales comments** — do not paraphrase vaguely. Use direct references: "Sales agent noted: '...'"
